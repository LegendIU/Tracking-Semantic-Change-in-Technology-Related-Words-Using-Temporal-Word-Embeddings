Motivation

Word meanings change over time as society, technology, media, and culture evolve. Static word embeddings represent each word with a single vector and cannot directly capture diachronic semantic change.

This project uses temporal word embeddings to analyze how selected words changed their distributional contexts between the 2010s and the 2020s in news texts.

Research Questions

  1. Can temporal word embeddings capture semantic shifts in news language?
  2. Which selected words show the strongest semantic change?
  3. Do target words shift more than stable control words?
  4. Are the shifts interpretable through nearest-neighbor analysis?

Dataset

Dataset: News Category Dataset

The dataset contains news headlines, short descriptions, categories, and publication dates. In this project, the headline and short description were combined into a single text field. The publication date was used to extract the year.

Separate Word2Vec Skip-gram models were trained for each time period. Since independently trained embeddings have different coordinate systems, the spaces were aligned using Orthogonal Procrustes alignment. Semantic shift was measured as cosine distance from the base period.

Interpretation

  • model showed the strongest shift, suggesting a change in contexts where models are discussed.
  • network shifted toward media, political, business, and public communication contexts.
  • platform shifted toward digital, media, social, and communication contexts.
  • virus shifted toward disease and pandemic-related contexts in the 2020s, likely due to COVID-19 coverage.

Target vs. Control Finding

The difference between target and control words was modest. Therefore, the experiment does not fully confirm that all selected target words shift more than control words on average.

Individual words such as model, network, platform, and virus showed clear and interpretable semantic shifts.

Limitations

  • The dataset represents news-domain language, not general English.
  • The 2020s period is much smaller than the 2010s period.
  • Only two broad time periods were used.
  • Word2Vec can be unstable on smaller or imbalanced corpora.
  • Semantic shifts may reflect news topic changes, not only meaning change.
  • Some candidate words were too rare or produced weak results.

Conclusion

This project demonstrates that temporal word embeddings can be used to detect and visualize semantic change in a news corpus. The strongest shifts were found for model, network, platform, and virus.

Nearest-neighbor analysis suggests that these shifts are interpretable and connected to changes in news topics, especially digital media, public communication, and pandemic-related coverage.

However, the average difference between target and control words was modest. This highlights the importance of corpus balance, word frequency, and qualitative interpretation in diachronic semantic analysis.
