Tracking Semantic Change in Technology-Related Words Using Temporal Word Embeddings

Galkin Vladislav
News Category Dataset · Word2Vec Skip-gram · Orthogonal Procrustes Alignment · Streamlit Demo
Motivation
Word meanings change over time because technology, society, and media evolve. Static word embeddings represent each word with a single vector and cannot directly capture diachronic semantic change.
This project uses temporal Word2Vec embeddings to analyze how selected words changed their contexts between the 2010s and the 2020s in news texts.
Dataset and Experimental Setup
Dataset

News Category Dataset

News headlines and short descriptions were merged into one text field. Publication dates were converted into years.

Methodology
Pipeline

Corpus → Preprocessing → Time slicing → Word2Vec training → Procrustes alignment → Cosine shift → Nearest neighbors

Separate embeddings were trained for each period. Since independently trained vector spaces are not directly comparable, the spaces were aligned using Orthogonal Procrustes. Semantic shift was measured as cosine distance from the base period.

Interpretation and Discussion
  • model showed the strongest semantic movement.
  • network shifted toward media, political, business, and communication contexts.
  • platform shifted toward digital, media, social, and communication contexts.
  • virus shifted toward disease and pandemic-related contexts in the 2020s.
Target vs. Control

The target-control difference was modest. Therefore, the main conclusion is based on strong individual word shifts rather than a large group-level effect.

Conclusions and Limitations
Conclusions
  • Temporal Word2Vec embeddings successfully captured interpretable semantic shifts in news texts.
  • The strongest changes were observed for model, network, platform, and virus.
  • Nearest-neighbor analysis supports the cosine-distance measurements.
  • Semantic change can be quantitatively tracked across time slices using aligned embeddings.
Limitations
  • The corpus reflects news language rather than general English usage.
  • The 2020s subset is smaller than the 2010s subset.
  • Only two broad time periods were analyzed.
  • Some observed shifts may reflect topic drift rather than pure semantic drift.
